Design content

"The most critical element of design is, does it solve the problem? Does it produce the desired outcome?" (Accidental Instructional Designer, p. 30)

There many options for the way you can go about your design. And generally, they all consist of steps that take you from an idea to a finished product. But what happens in between, and how you go about achieving it is key!

The design approach you choose, will dictate the specific steps you'll take towards the completion of your product. Whether you use ADDIE, LXD, or another model (more on those below), it is important to use a model so that your activities and interactions are effective; plus, it gives you a structure to follow and helps keep you on track! Whatever name you want to call it, know that there are many ways to go about your design. 


Generally, all models consist of similar approaches that will support the designer through:
analysis and planning
design and development
testing and prototyping
implementation
evaluation

We're sharing here several models for you to look at and decide which suits your needs best. You can explore different Design Methods such as ADDIE and Learning Experience Design (LXD).

Make sure to also visit the section about learning products and experiences you can produce, and remember to take a look at the responsibilities for the design section also featured on this page. By the end, you’ll be set and ready to tackle your product design!

ADDIE or LXD? The choice is yours.

Instructional Design (ID), is perhaps the most-known design process for learning design. Simply put, “instructional design is the process by which learning products and experiences are designed, developed, and delivered. These learning products include online courses, instructional manuals, video tutorials, learning simulations, etc. Instructional designers are the 'architects' of the learning experience and the 'directors' of the [design] process” (cite). Most often, when we think of ID, we automatically think of ADDIE (analysis, design, development, implementation, evaluation).

Learning Experience Design (LXD) is somewhat a newer concept when it comes to design. Although there are many definitions of LXD out there, generally, this process is deemed to be more human-centered and goal-oriented than instructional design. This process focuses on designing for the user by considering their needs and wants, behaviours, goals, the process that the learner goes through, and so on. LXD incorporates Design Thinking and User Experience practices which allow the designer “to understand the user, challenge assumptions, and redefine problems in an attempt to identify alternative strategies and solutions that might not be instantly apparent with our initial level of understanding” (cite).

Learning products and experiences

Congratulations! You’ve completed your analysis phase and have started mapping out your findings and ideas. This section covers some of the different learning products and experiences that you can produce. Remember that these are just some examples, so don’t limit yourself to them; the sky’s the limit! (Playbook)

In addition to the options discussed in the accordions below the old LSD team worked on a placemat that describes many more learning products and experience options. This placemat will also give you an idea of the level of effort, cost and time required to develop the different products.

Job aids

Job aids are short, online resources designed to answer very specific learning needs of limited scope. They are usually presented as short documents, videos, posters or graphics that present the information in a succinct, accessible way for quick reference by the user.

Instructor led training - Classroom

Classroom products deliver content in-person, with a qualified instructor delivering to a group of learners. The instructor can use slides, videos, posters and other physical learning material to deliver the course. Courses can be delivered in Ottawa or throughout the regions.

Instructor led training - Virtual Classroom

Virtual classrooms integrate instructor-led learning into a distance, online learning platform. Using software such as Moodle, instructors can hold virtual classes remotely through webchat, live chat or other software with learners from different locations, while also providing assignments and feedback and tracking attendance using tools built into the software. If you have any questions about the different tools available to you for virtual classroom delivery, please visit the section called "Virtual Classroom Tools", available from the main page of the Playbook.

Online self-paced

Online self-paced products offer courses that can be completed on a web browser through a computer or mobile device. The learner sets the pace of their learning through the course, and can stop and restart at any time. The course is completed once certain benchmarks are reached, such as successfully passing assessments embedded in the product. Multimedia such as videos, images, external links and graphics can also be integrated into the product.

Blended learning

Blended learning integrates both online and in-person learning into one product. The sequence of delivery varies from product to product: some courses may have the completion of the online section as a prerequisite for in-class attendance, whereas another product may intersperse online learning over the course of the in-person class.

Distance learning

Learners log into an online learning environment (e.g., Moodle) to access course content, blogs, wikis, quizzes and homework assignments on their own time. Distance learning provides a social learning space where learners and the instructor can interact through threaded discussions. Typically, courses are led by an instructor and delivered over a specific period of time (e.g. two weeks).

Visit each one of the sections to learn about the aspects that you need to include as part of your product design.

Accessibility is not just a check in a box. It's what allows us to be inclusive with all types of learners, who are in all types of disability situations. We want to make sure that all learners can learn what they need to do their jobs well, and, as such, remove the barriers that would prevent some of us as public servants to thrive in our careers.
The Accessibility team is there to support you in developing your understanding and your skills when it comes to applying accessibility so that the learning experiences that you create meet the standards in place. We hold information sessions, offer job aids, and we can help you get unstuck within a particular project. We are fierce advocates for learners who have disabilities and we are passionate about bringing meaningful learning experiences to every public servant.
Accessible Education is the process of designing courses and developing a teaching style to meet the needs of people from a variety of backgrounds and abilities.

Analyzing issues from a gender and diversity perspective is a competency. Gender-based Analysis Plus (GBA+) helps to ensure the consideration and inclusion of the experiences of diverse groups of people in the development of federal initiatives, including training. Take some time to familiarize yourself with the ways to integrate GBA+ into instructional design.

If you are interested and want to learn more, also consider completing the Introduction to Gender-based Analysis Plus (GBA+) course, a 2-hour introductory online course. For Guide for CSPS Employees on GBA+, visit the School’s GCdocs link, or access the documents here.

As a federal organization that defends the values of bilingualism, the School commits to honouring its legal obligations under the Official Languages Act. For all your questions and needs related to OL at CSPS, we invite you to visit the School’s Official Languages intranet page. For best official languages practices, take some time to familiarize yourself with the Official Languages Standard, as well as the Guide to Best Practices for learning product design –or you can check out this quick Job Aid as a shortcut to the Guide.

Issues surrounding copyright are often very nuanced, complex, and specific to particular circumstances. The CSPS Copyright Guide provides general information about copyright and how it impacts the content of the School’s learning product.
